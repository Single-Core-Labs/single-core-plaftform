RESEARCH PAPER
Medical Imaging in the Age of Artificial Intelligence:
Advances, Architectures, and the Road to Clinical Translation
Akshita Singh
May 2025

ABSTRACT
Medical imaging sits at the intersection of clinical urgency and computational innovation. Over the past decade, the field has been profoundly reshaped by machine learning and deep learning, evolving from hand-crafted feature pipelines to end-to-end neural architectures that match or surpass expert-level performance across numerous diagnostic tasks. This paper provides a comprehensive, current review of the medical imaging landscape as of 2025, covering the fundamental modalities (X-ray, CT, MRI, ultrasound, PET), the dominant AI/ML paradigms (convolutional networks, vision transformers, foundation models, and generative AI), and the emerging frontier of multimodal and vision-language systems. We examine quantitative performance benchmarks, clinical deployment case studies, and the growing body of evidence on real-world efficacy. Critically, we address the unsolved challenges that lie between research excellence and clinical adoption: dataset bias, explainability, regulatory pathways, and workflow integration. The paper concludes with a forward-looking perspective on where the field is heading, including agentic AI systems, federated learning, and patient-centric imaging intelligence.
Keywords: medical imaging, deep learning, convolutional neural networks, vision transformers, foundation models, generative AI, radiology, explainable AI, federated learning, clinical translation

 

1. Introduction
   Medical imaging is the cornerstone of modern diagnostics. From detecting a solitary pulmonary nodule on a chest radiograph to delineating a glioblastoma on a multiparametric MRI, imaging provides the clinician with irreplaceable windows into the human body. Globally, more than 3.6 billion diagnostic imaging examinations are performed each year, producing a volume of visual data that no radiologist workforce can fully interpret alone.
   The confluence of three forces has made artificial intelligence (AI) indispensable to this field: the explosion of digital imaging data (driven by PACS adoption and electronic health records), the dramatic gains in GPU-accelerated deep learning, and the maturation of open-source frameworks and annotated public datasets. These forces have catalysed a paradigm shift, moving medical image analysis from rule-based expert systems to data-driven neural networks capable of complex perceptual reasoning.
   As of mid-2025, the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) has cleared over 1,250 AI-enabled medical devices, up from approximately 950 in August 2024, with radiology-based tools comprising the overwhelming majority. The global AI-enabled medical device market was valued at $13.7 billion in 2024 and is projected to surpass $255 billion by 2033. The question is no longer whether AI will transform medical imaging, but how quickly, how safely, and how equitably that transformation will occur.
   This paper is structured as follows. Section 2 reviews the primary imaging modalities and their computational characteristics. Section 3 surveys the architectural evolution of AI models for imaging, from early CNNs to the current generation of vision-language foundation models. Section 4 examines specific clinical applications with emphasis on performance benchmarks. Section 5 addresses generative AI. Section 6 analyses the challenge of clinical translation, including explainability, bias, and regulation. Section 7 offers a forward-looking synthesis.

2. Imaging Modalities: A Computational Perspective
   Before examining AI architectures, it is essential to understand the distinct physical, informational, and computational properties of the major imaging modalities. Each modality presents a unique set of challenges and opportunities for machine learning.
   2.1 Radiography (X-Ray)
   Plain radiography remains the most widely used diagnostic imaging modality worldwide, owing to its low cost, high throughput, and near-universal availability. Chest X-ray (CXR) is the single most common imaging examination, accounting for roughly 40% of all radiological studies. From an AI standpoint, CXR datasets are the best-resourced in the field: databases such as CheXpert (224,316 images), MIMIC-CXR (227,827 studies), and VinDr-CXR provide large-scale labelled training corpora.
   The computational challenge of radiography lies in its 2D projection nature: overlapping anatomical structures create ambiguity that even experienced radiologists find difficult to resolve. AI models must therefore learn not just local texture features but global contextual relationships. The CheXpert labelling system defined 14 pathological labels, and state-of-the-art models now achieve area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUROC) scores exceeding 0.90 on several of these classes—matching or exceeding radiologist-level performance on individual findings.
   2.2 Computed Tomography (CT)
   CT is the workhorse of emergency and oncological imaging, producing high-resolution 3D volumetric data through the acquisition of multiple X-ray projections at different angles. The volumetric nature of CT presents a qualitatively different computational problem: models must reason across hundreds of axial slices, integrating information in three spatial dimensions.
   Lung nodule detection, aortic dissection classification, intracranial haemorrhage triage, and COVID-19 severity scoring are among the highest-impact AI CT applications. Deep learning models for CT have benefited from the development of 3D convolutions, volumetric attention mechanisms, and, more recently, CT-specific foundation models. In 2024, researchers demonstrated the first generalist foundation model trained on a multimodal dataset for 3D CT, representing a significant step toward task-agnostic CT analysis.
   2.3 Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI)
   MRI is the modality of choice for soft-tissue contrast, neurological assessment, and musculoskeletal imaging. Its rich multi-sequence nature (T1, T2, FLAIR, DWI, DCE, etc.) provides complementary tissue characterisation that no single channel can offer alone. This multi-sequence structure maps naturally onto multi-input neural network architectures, and fusion of MRI sequences has driven state-of-the-art performance in brain tumour segmentation.
   A major 2025 development is the emergence of MRI-specific foundation models. Sun et al. (Nature Biomedical Engineering, 2025) described a foundation model for enhancing MRI images and improving downstream segmentation, registration, and diagnostic tasks. Concurrently, diffusion-model-based temporal super-resolution frameworks demonstrated the ability to reconstruct high-temporal-resolution 4D MRI from undersampled acquisitions, with direct implications for cardiac and fetal imaging.
   MRI acquisition is also uniquely amenable to AI-accelerated reconstruction. Undersampled k-space data can be reconstructed by deep learning with clinically acceptable fidelity at acceleration factors of 4-8x, dramatically reducing scan times. This addresses one of MRI's core clinical limitations: long acquisition duration.
   2.4 Ultrasound
   Ultrasound is real-time, portable, ionising-radiation-free, and inexpensive, making it the modality of choice in resource-limited settings, obstetrics, point-of-care diagnosis, and interventional guidance. Its main limitation for AI is high operator dependence and image-quality variability across devices and sonographers.
   Recent work has demonstrated that deep learning models can substantially reduce this variability. Hernandez Torres et al. (2024, Bioengineering) evaluated deep learning architectures for point-of-care ultrasound diagnostics, showing that carefully architected CNNs can achieve performance comparable to board-certified sonographers on standard cardiac views. The push toward AI-guided ultrasound acquisition, where the model provides real-time feedback to guide non-expert operators, represents one of the most democratising applications in the field.
   2.5 Nuclear Medicine: PET and SPECT
   Positron emission tomography (PET) is a functional imaging modality that images metabolic processes, most commonly using 18F-FDG to visualise glucose uptake in malignant tissue. PET is inherently noisy and low-resolution, typically co-registered with CT (PET/CT) or MRI (PET/MRI) for anatomical reference. AI methods have targeted PET image enhancement (denoising, super-resolution), standardised uptake value (SUV) quantification, and end-to-end lesion detection pipelines.
   The integration of PET imaging into multimodal AI frameworks—combining structural CT/MRI with functional PET information and genomic or proteomic data—is now being actively pursued for precision oncology, particularly in lymphoma staging and treatment response assessment.

3. The Architectural Evolution of AI in Medical Imaging
   3.1 Convolutional Neural Networks: The First Wave
   The resurgence of deep learning in medical imaging can be traced to the publication of AlexNet (2012) and its dramatic victory at the ImageNet Large Scale Visual Recognition Challenge. By 2016-2018, purpose-built architectures such as U-Net had been developed for medical image segmentation, exploiting skip connections to preserve spatial detail across encoder-decoder pathways. U-Net and its successors (U-Net++, nnU-Net) remain highly competitive baselines and are still widely deployed in production clinical pipelines.
   CNNs are defined by local receptive fields and translational equivariance—properties that align naturally with the texture-rich, spatially structured nature of medical images. Their inductive biases make them data-efficient, a critical advantage in medicine where annotated data is scarce. However, CNNs struggle to model long-range spatial dependencies: a finding on one side of a chest X-ray may clinically constrain interpretation on the other side, but a standard CNN cannot capture this without deep stacking of layers. This limitation motivated the adoption of attention mechanisms and transformer architectures.
   3.2 Vision Transformers and Attention-Based Models
   The Vision Transformer (ViT; Dosovitskiy et al., 2020) applied the transformer's self-attention mechanism to image patches, enabling models to capture arbitrary long-range dependencies regardless of spatial distance. Azad et al. (Medical Image Analysis, 2024) published a comprehensive review of advances in medical image analysis with vision transformers, documenting their superiority over CNNs on tasks requiring global context, such as whole-slide pathology classification and 3D volumetric tumour delineation.
   The Swin Transformer introduced hierarchical windowed self-attention, reducing the quadratic complexity of global attention while preserving expressiveness. It has become one of the dominant backbones for medical imaging tasks, particularly in 3D settings where computational cost is prohibitive for plain ViT. Hybrid CNN-ViT architectures—using CNN stems for local feature extraction and transformer layers for global reasoning—have emerged as the dominant design paradigm for high-performance medical imaging models as of 2025.
   Spotlight: Segment Anything Model (SAM) in Medicine
   Meta AI's Segment Anything Model (SAM), released in 2023, established a new paradigm: a single promptable segmentation model applicable to any image. Medical adaptations (MedSAM, SAM-Med2D, SAM-Med3D) fine-tuned SAM on curated medical corpora, demonstrating generalised organ and lesion segmentation across CT, MRI, and pathology slides. Zhang et al. (Computers in Biology and Medicine, 2024) systematically reviewed SAM's medical image segmentation applications, concluding that while SAM adaptations underperform task-specific models on narrow benchmarks, their zero-shot generalisability is clinically transformative—enabling rapid deployment without the need for large labelled datasets in each new domain.

3.3 Foundation Models: Towards Generalist Medical AI
Foundation models—large neural networks pre-trained on broad data and adapted to downstream tasks via fine-tuning or prompting—represent the most significant architectural shift in medical imaging since deep learning itself. The seminal 2023 Nature paper by Moor et al. articulated the case for generalist medical AI based on foundation models, arguing that the field had been hampered by the proliferation of narrow, task-specific models that do not generalise, cannot share learned representations, and require prohibitive annotation effort for each new clinical application.
By 2024-2025, this vision has materialised into several concrete systems. A foundation model for enhancing MRI images and supporting downstream segmentation, registration, and diagnostic tasks was published in Nature Biomedical Engineering (Sun et al., 2025). In the ophthalmology domain, the first specialty-specific foundation model was released in 2024, demonstrating that foundation models can be built from clinically curated, modality-specific data at manageable scale.
For 3D CT, Hamamci et al. (2024) described the development of generalist foundation models trained on large-scale multimodal CT datasets, achieving competitive performance on anatomical segmentation, lesion detection, and report generation without task-specific fine-tuning. The AI4HI network's position paper (2025) outlined community consensus on the design principles and evaluation criteria for radiology foundation models—standardising how these systems should be benchmarked and validated.
3.4 Vision-Language Models and Multimodal AI
The integration of imaging and natural language has opened an entirely new frontier. Vision-language models (VLMs) are trained on paired image-text data—in medicine, typically radiological images and their corresponding reports—enabling bidirectional querying: generating reports from images (automated radiology reporting) and retrieving images from textual queries (cross-modal retrieval). A 2025 review in Biomedical Engineering Letters systematically examined VLMs from 2022-2024 across modality-specific applications, documenting rapid progress in report generation quality and cross-modal retrieval accuracy.
Multi-modal AI has demonstrated strong potential for increasing the accuracy of disease risk assessment and stratification, as well as monitoring key cardiovascular and metabolic risk drivers. By integrating imaging modalities such as CT, MRI, and PET with genomic and proteomic data, these systems provide comprehensive information for tumour detection, staging, and personalised treatment planning. The fusion of histopathological slide data and genomic information can further reveal the molecular characteristics of tumours, enabling precision medicine at the tissue level.
Nature published a dedicated study in 2025 examining vision-language foundation models for 3D medical imaging, demonstrating that joint visual-textual pre-training on large volumetric datasets produces representations that transfer far more effectively to downstream tasks than vision-only pre-training alone—particularly in data-limited scenarios.

4. Clinical Applications and Performance Benchmarks
   4.1 Cancer Detection and Screening
   Oncological imaging represents the highest-stakes application of AI in radiology. Lung cancer is the leading cause of cancer mortality worldwide, and low-dose CT (LDCT) screening has demonstrated mortality reduction in high-risk populations. AI models for lung nodule detection now routinely achieve sensitivities exceeding 95% at clinically acceptable false-positive rates, enabling fully automated, rule-based referral pathways in some health systems.
   Breast imaging has seen substantial AI integration in mammography screening, with several AI-assisted reading systems receiving FDA and CE clearance. Meta-analyses published through 2025 show that AI assistance reduces radiologist reading time by 30-50% while maintaining or improving sensitivity for screen-detected cancers. The UK's NHSBSP AI pilot programme demonstrated non-inferiority of AI-assisted single reading compared to double reading—a finding with major workforce implications given the global shortage of radiologists.
   In pathology, AI models analyse whole-slide images (WSIs) of tissue specimens at gigapixel scale. Transformer-based architectures and attention mechanisms now support end-to-end predictions combining the strengths of microscopic and macroscopic views. A deep learning model published in The Lancet demonstrated AUROC >0.99 for prostate cancer Gleason grading on internal validation—though real-world generalisation remains an active area of investigation.
   4.2 Neuroimaging and Brain Disorders
   Brain MRI analysis encompasses one of the most complex and heterogeneous problems in medical imaging. Coluzzi et al. (Bioengineering, 2025) applied explainable artificial intelligence to multiple MRI-derived brain measures for Alzheimer's disease classification, demonstrating not only competitive diagnostic accuracy but also meaningful biomarker identification—enabling the model to implicate specific brain regions associated with neurodegeneration. FundusNet (Hu et al., Bioengineering, 2025) extended this multimodal approach to retinal fundus images, achieving rapid diagnosis of neurodegenerative and eye diseases from a single non-invasive imaging modality.
   Multiple sclerosis (MS) lesion segmentation and longitudinal change detection are areas where AI has moved rapidly toward clinical deployment. Automated lesion tracking reduces inter-reader variability from ~30% to <5%, enabling far more sensitive monitoring of disease activity and treatment response. For acute stroke, AI-based CT perfusion analysis achieves door-to-treatment time reductions of 15-30 minutes in several health systems—a difference that directly translates to reduced neurological deficit.
   4.3 Cardiovascular Imaging
   Cardiac AI encompasses automated chamber quantification from echocardiography, AI-accelerated cardiac MRI reconstruction, coronary artery calcium scoring from CT, and haemodynamic risk prediction from coronary CT angiography. Wu et al. (Diagnostics, 2024) described a deep learning approach based on the UNeXt architecture for automatic left ventricular segmentation in transesophageal echocardiography, achieving competitive Dice scores versus manual expert delineation.
   A breakthrough application is automated detection and differentiation of Stanford Type A and Type B aortic dissections from CTA scans using deep learning, described in a 2025 Diagnostics publication. Given that Type A dissection carries a mortality of 1-2% per hour without surgery, rapid AI triage of this time-critical diagnosis could directly save lives.
   4.4 Opportunistic Screening and Incidental Findings
   A compelling emerging use case is opportunistic AI screening: applying AI to imaging studies performed for unrelated clinical indications to detect incidental findings of high public health value. Kim et al. (Diagnostics, 2024) demonstrated automated deep learning detection of acute vertebral fractures on routine abdominal or chest CT scans, identifying osteoporotic fractures that would otherwise go unreported. Similar opportunistic approaches have been demonstrated for coronary artery calcium scoring on lung cancer screening CTs, hepatic steatosis assessment on abdominal CTs, and bone density estimation.
   Opportunistic AI screening has the potential to convert the existing enormous volume of routine imaging into a population-level health surveillance infrastructure, at virtually zero additional cost or patient burden.

5. Generative AI in Medical Imaging
   5.1 The Generative AI Landscape
   Generative AI has emerged as a transformative force in medical imaging, offering capabilities in data synthesis, image enhancement, modality translation, and spatiotemporal modelling. Three architectural families dominate the landscape:
6. Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) — composed of competing generator and discriminator networks, producing high-fidelity synthetic images and enabling modality translation (e.g., MRI-to-CT synthesis).
7. Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) — latent-space models enabling structured image generation and representation learning.
8. Diffusion Models — iterative denoising generative models that now surpass GANs in image quality and diversity, with strong theoretical grounding. They have emerged as the dominant paradigm for high-quality medical image synthesis as of 2024-2025.

5.2 Synthetic Data for Training Augmentation
The scarcity of annotated medical imaging data is a fundamental bottleneck for AI development. Generative models address this through synthetic data augmentation. GANs have established themselves as particularly robust in synthesising realistic medical images near real-world clinical data. A 2025 study in EBioMedicine systematically examined the potential of synthetic data in medical imaging research, finding that when synthetic images were used as augmentation on minority classes, downstream classifier performance improved significantly—particularly for rare pathologies with inherently imbalanced datasets.
Diffusion models now enable more controlled and semantically coherent image synthesis. Anatomy-aware diffusion models can generate surgical scene images with specified anatomical structures, supporting training of surgical AI systems where collection of real data is impractical. Xu et al. (Research, 2024) developed a framework for generating synthetic breast ultrasound images to address medical data sharing barriers, demonstrating that synthetic data from diffusion models can substitute for real data in certain training regimes without compromising downstream model performance.
5.3 Cross-Modality Synthesis and Translation
Cross-modality synthesis—computationally generating one imaging modality from another—has both practical and scientific value. Clinically, it can reduce patient radiation dose (by generating CT-like images from MRI), shorten scan times, and enable retrospective harmonisation of multi-site imaging data. Scientifically, synthesis models that faithfully translate between modalities must encode deep structural understanding of anatomy and tissue physics.
Cycle-consistent GANs (CycleGANs) were the initial dominant approach for unpaired image-to-image translation. More recently, diffusion models conditioned on source-modality images have produced sharper, more anatomically consistent translations. RoentGen, a vision-language foundation model for chest X-ray generation, demonstrated that textual reports could be used to condition X-ray synthesis—opening the door to generating training examples for rare radiological findings described in text but sparsely present in real datasets.
5.4 Prognostic and Predictive Imaging
Perhaps the most clinically impactful application of generative AI is not synthesis per se, but predictive temporal modelling. Liu et al. (2025) demonstrated an MRI-based tumour growth prediction framework achieving SSIM of 0.92 and PSNR of 29.0 dB for multiparametric MRI generation over treatment timescales. Kyung et al. (2024) achieved a weighted macro AUROC of 0.72 in predicting future chest X-ray pathologies from electronic health record data—essentially enabling AI to anticipate radiological deterioration before it becomes visible.
These predictive imaging systems represent a paradigm shift from reactive diagnosis to proactive prognosis, with clear implications for early intervention, treatment planning, and personalised medicine.

6. Challenges in Clinical Translation
   6.1 Data Bias and Generalisation
   The most persistent challenge in clinical AI is generalisation: a model trained at one institution frequently underperforms when deployed at another. Even an AI tool with 95% accuracy in one hospital may drop to approximately 80% elsewhere if imaging protocols differ (e.g., CT scanner models, patient positioning, reconstruction kernels). This generalisation gap stems from dataset bias—the model learning spurious correlations with acquisition parameters, patient demographics, or local annotation practices rather than the underlying pathophysiology.
   Federated learning has emerged as a promising technical solution, enabling models to be trained across distributed institutions without sharing patient data. By aggregating model gradients rather than raw images, federated approaches can train on the full diversity of multi-site data while preserving privacy. Multiple federated learning consortia in radiology have demonstrated that federated models achieve performance comparable to centralised models on held-out datasets, while exhibiting superior robustness to distribution shift.
   6.2 Explainability and Clinical Trust
   Deep learning models are frequently characterised as 'black boxes': they produce predictions without legible justifications. This opacity is a barrier to clinical adoption on multiple fronts. Clinicians are reluctant to act on recommendations they cannot interrogate; regulators require evidence of safety and reliability that opaque models cannot easily provide; and medico-legal liability frameworks presuppose that decisions can be explained and audited.
   Explainable AI (XAI) methods including gradient-based saliency maps, class activation mapping (CAM), SHAP values, and attention visualisation attempt to render model decisions interpretable. Grad-CAM and its variants have been widely adopted for medical imaging, superimposing heatmaps over input images to indicate which regions drove a classification. However, recent empirical studies have found that radiologists often find these saliency maps clinically unhelpful: the highlighted regions frequently correspond to plausible-but-unintended features rather than the actual diagnostic criterion the clinician would use.
   The challenge for the field is not merely to produce post-hoc explanations of black-box models, but to design models whose reasoning is inherently aligned with clinical logic. Concept-based explanations, prototype networks, and reasoning-chain approaches inspired by large language model techniques represent emerging directions that may close this gap.
   6.3 Regulatory Pathways
   The FDA's regulatory framework for AI-enabled medical devices has evolved substantially. As of August 2024, the FDA cleared 97% of AI-enabled devices via the 510(k) pathway, which requires demonstrating substantial equivalence to a predicate device rather than clinical superiority. The most recent FDA draft guidance, published January 2025, proposes lifecycle management considerations and specific recommendations for marketing submissions of AI-enabled medical devices.
   A critical concern identified in regulatory analyses is that most AI device summaries lack basic information study design, sample size, and demographic breakdown making post-market safety assessment difficult. By mid-2025, only approximately 5% of cleared AI devices had reported adverse-event data. This paucity of post-market surveillance is compounded by the adaptive nature of many AI systems: unlike static drugs, AI models can be updated, retrained, and modified post-clearance in ways that alter their behaviour.
   The FDA's Predetermined Change Control Plan (PCCP) framework, formalised in the 2021 AI/ML Software Action Plan, attempts to address this by allowing manufacturers to pre-specify the parameters within which algorithm updates can occur without triggering new submissions. The EU AI Act, which will require radiology AI tools to meet 'high-risk' compliance requirements by 2026, including documentation of training data curation, bias checks, and human oversight policies, represents the most stringent regulatory framework yet applied to AI medical devices globally.
   6.4 Workflow Integration and Human-AI Collaboration
   Technical performance is a necessary but insufficient condition for clinical impact. AI tools must integrate into existing clinical workflows without adding cognitive burden, increasing alert fatigue, or disrupting established clinical roles. Studies of AI integration in radiology have documented the 'automation bias' phenomenon: radiologists may defer excessively to AI recommendations, accepting AI errors they would have caught independently. Conversely, 'algorithm aversion' can lead clinicians to dismiss valid AI findings.
   The optimal model of human-AI collaboration in radiology remains an active research question. Current evidence supports 'second reader' configurations—where AI flags potentially abnormal studies for priority review—over fully autonomous AI reading, particularly for high-stakes diagnoses. However, for triage functions (identifying urgent cases in a queue), autonomous AI operation with human oversight has demonstrated clear time-to-treatment benefits.

7. Future Directions and Emerging Frontiers
   7.1 Agentic and Orchestrated AI Systems
   The next wave of medical AI is moving beyond single-task models toward agentic systems that can reason, plan, and orchestrate multiple tools to complete complex clinical workflows. An agentic radiology AI might autonomously retrieve relevant prior imaging, query laboratory results from the EHR, apply multiple specialist models (lung nodule, incidental liver lesion, vertebral fracture), and synthesise these outputs into a structured report—all without step-by-step human prompting. Large language models (LLMs) with tool-use capabilities provide the orchestration layer for such systems. Pellegrino et al. (Diagnostics, 2024) evaluated GPT-4 for colonoscopy Boston Bowel Preparation scoring, demonstrating that conversational LLMs can perform structured clinical scoring tasks with concordance comparable to trained endoscopists.
   7.2 Liquid Biopsy and Radiomics Integration
   Radiomics—the high-throughput extraction of quantitative features from medical images—is being combined with liquid biopsy (circulating tumour DNA, exosomes, and cell-free RNA) to create multi-omic predictive models. 'Radiomic signatures' that correlate with tumour mutation status, immunotherapy response, and resistance mechanisms are moving from discovery to prospective clinical validation. The fusion of histopathological slide data and genomic information can reveal molecular characteristics of tumours, enabling precision personalised treatment that transcends what either imaging or genomics can provide alone.
   7.3 Point-of-Care and Low-Resource AI
   The global burden of undiagnosed disease is concentrated in low- and middle-income countries (LMICs), where access to specialist radiologists and high-field MRI is severely limited. Portable ultrasound devices with integrated AI now enable trained community health workers to perform diagnostic quality cardiac and obstetric imaging in rural settings. AI models optimised for point-of-care ultrasound—requiring only commodity smartphone compute rather than GPU servers—are among the highest-equity applications in the field.
   AI-driven mammography triage for LMICs, where double-reading is unavailable, could enable population-level breast cancer screening programmes that would otherwise be financially and logistically impossible. Similarly, AI analysis of chest X-rays for tuberculosis triage has been validated in prospective studies in sub-Saharan Africa, demonstrating sensitivity and specificity competitive with radiologist reading.
   7.4 Patient-Centric and Longitudinal Imaging AI
   Current AI tools are largely episode-centric: they analyse a single imaging study in isolation. The next generation will be longitudinal, integrating each new imaging study into an evolving patient-specific model that tracks disease trajectory, predicts future imaging findings, and contextualises new findings against the full temporal history of the patient's imaging record. Generative models capable of predicting tumour growth trajectories and future chest X-ray morphology represent early examples of this paradigm.
   Patient-facing AI is also expected to expand. LLM-mediated report simplification can convert radiological jargon into plain-language summaries accessible to patients, enabling more informed and participatory healthcare. This patient-centric direction aligns with broader trends toward personalised and preventive medicine.

8. Conclusion
   Medical imaging stands at an extraordinary inflection point. The last five years have witnessed the emergence of transformer architectures, foundation models, generative AI, and vision-language systems that collectively represent a step-change in what AI can achieve in medical image analysis. Performance benchmarks that once seemed distant—radiologist-level lung cancer detection, automated aortic dissection triage, predictive imaging of tumour growth—are now documented realities.
   Yet the path from benchmark to bedside remains demanding. Generalisation across sites and populations, clinician trust, regulatory clarity, and equitable access are not technical problems that neural network architectures can solve alone. They require interdisciplinary collaboration between engineers, clinicians, ethicists, regulators, and patients.
   What is clear is that the trajectory is irreversible. With over 1,250 FDA-cleared AI devices and a market projected to exceed $255 billion within a decade, AI-augmented medical imaging is no longer a research horizon—it is a clinical present. The task for the medical and technical community is to ensure that this transformation is rigorous, safe, explainable, and above all, genuinely beneficial to patients.
